Context

What did I learn over the 10 week research experience?


Over this past summer, I worked as an UX researcher at the University of Michigan-Flint for the National Science Foundation (NSF) Research Experience for Undergraduates (REU). I joined a team of 3 student researchers with Dr. Zytko being our mentor.

During the course of 10 weeks alongside my team, I applied for the Institutional Review Board (IRB), analyzed 10+ literature reviews, created an 80 question Qualtrics survey flow, and analyzed 50+ responses with reflexive thematic analysis (RTA).

We designed this study to answer...

1. How do the visually and aurally impaired exchange consent from online to in person dating environments?

2. Are there notable differences in how individuals with these impairments exchange consent compared to those without?

**Due to the confidentiality of this project, I cannot present the specific data collected.*

Study Plan

What was our original goal?


In the second week, our goal was to recruit at least 20 participants, finalize our study process, and submit the IRB application. However, this turned out to be more challenging than anticipated and became a major obstacle for our project.

This experience taught me that flexibility is key in research, as we had to adapt to unexpected challenges.


What did we accomplish?


We completed most of our tasks...

— Defined our recruitment criteria (e.g. impariment type, dating experience)
— Determined Prolific as our recruitment platform to recruit
— Designed an online survey for our approach
— completed the PEERRS Certification (IRB requirement)
— Finalized our study process

Initially, we considered conducting an in-person workshop or a hybrid study. However, given the specific criteria and constraints, we realized that meeting participants in person might be challenging due to various constraints, especially within our tight timeframe.



What happened to the IRB?


We didn’t receive approval until 5 weeks later. This delay was a major setback as we had to adjust our timeline and recruitment strategy. But we adapted by continuously modifying the application and refining our survey structure during this period..




Analysis

Process

How did we choose our appraoch?


We reviewed additional literature and explored the three schools of thematic analysis. We concluded that RTA, with its emphasis on personal interpretation and expression, was the best fit for our project, as our unique perspectives provided a valuable advantage in analyzing the data.


How did we apply 6 stages of RTA to our survey?


1. Familiarize ourselves with data — While waiting for the responses, I planned our thematic analysis process with the team and tested it with 20 participantsto ensure clarity and quality. After the first iteration, we noticed some participants were confused by the questions, so we made adjustments. We also filtered out responses that didn’t address the questions appropriately.

2. Create a code book in Google Sheets — Having a total of 4 reserchers, we decided to analyze the data individually so we can form out own interpretations. Our plan was to review each piece of data in pairs, with three people examining each response. However, with 50 responses and over 80 questions, we found this approach too time-consuming. Instead, we adjusted our method and proceeded to the next step. .



3. Move codes to Miro for initial theming — After transferring each response to a sticky note, we categorized them by disability to identify any nuances within specific groups.



4. Check themes — We re-coded the responses individually but struggled to identify key themes. Our mentor suggested reading through the data and noting what stood out. As simple as that, we began to see patterns.


5. Finalize themes — From there, we refined our themes and connected each them to see how they related to one another, which helped us gain a clearer overall understanding.



6. Report on findings — We are still in the process of analyzing and aim to have a publication written.
